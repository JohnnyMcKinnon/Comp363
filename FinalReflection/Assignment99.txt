                                Final Reflection:
This semester has significantly improved my understanding of how algorithm design 
affects both theoretical efficiency and real-world performance. One of the most 
important things I learned was how different paradigms like divide-and-conquer 
or dynamic programming approaches problem solving in fundamentally different ways. 
Before this course, I understood recursion at a very basic level, but implementing 
algorithms like Karatsuba multiplication made me see how reducing even a single 
recursive call per level can dramatically change time complexity. Reducing the 
number of recursive multiplications from four to three lowers the time complexity 
from quadratic growth, and testing it with large inputs makes the performance 
difference much more concrete.
A part of the course I found interesting was comparing algorithms experimentally. 
Timing the divide-and-conquer multiplication against Karatsuba showed clearly that 
simpler algorithms can outperform more advanced ones at small input sizes due to 
overhead, but lose badly as input size grows. I had a similar experience when 
comparing Borůvka and Kruskal for minimum spanning trees. Seeing Borůvka’s 
implementation scale poorly due to repeated full-graph scans while Kruskal 
remained efficient helped reinforce the importance of data structures like 
union-find and the idea that implementation details matter just as much as 
high-level design. 
I also enjoyed working with graph algorithms like DFS for 
reachability and connected components, as well as shortest path algorithms in DAGs. 
These assignments helped me better understand traversal strategies and how to 
systematically explore structures like graphs. Additionally, implementing Huffman 
coding and sequence alignment introduced me to how algorithms are applied in areas 
like compression and bioinformatics, which made the course feel more relevant beyond 
pure theory.
That said, not every part of the course was equally engaging. Some of the more 
mechanical aspects, like working with adjacency matrices or carefully handling 
edge cases in implementations, felt repetitive at times. While I understand their 
importance, those sections were less interesting compared to the higher-level ideas 
or performance comparisons. However, working through those details did improve my 
ability to write correct and complete implementations.
In terms of attendance and participation, my attendance was consistent with only a 
few absences. I participated in class discussions and stayed focused for most of 
the semester. I completed all assignments and generally took the time to understand 
both my own solutions and the instructor’s. Reviewing my work against the provided 
solutions showed that my overall approach and understanding were strong, especially 
in terms of algorithm structure and correctness. Based on my consistent effort, full 
completion of assignments, and solid understanding of the material, I believe I have 
earned an A- in this course.
